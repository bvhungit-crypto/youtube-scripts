In nineteen forty-one, an SS officer started killing prisoners during his lunch breaks. Three years later, his own commanders would call him "excessive." What does that word mean in Auschwitz?
The question isn't rhetorical. It's the only way to understand what happened next.
Because when the machinery of genocide calls someone excessive, you're not looking at a monster. You're looking at something the system itself couldn't contain.
His name was Gerhard Palitzsch. Born nineteen ten, Dresden. Working-class family. Joined the Nazi Party at nineteen, early enough that it looked good on paper, late enough that no one handed him the ideology at birth. He signed up. He showed up. He climbed. The distinction would become important.
By nineteen forty, he'd made SS-Hauptscharführer. Rapportführer at Auschwitz I. Head of prisoner roll call. You stand in the yard at dawn. You count bodies. You write numbers in ledgers. You report discrepancies.
A desk job with a whip.
He arrived at Auschwitz in May nineteen forty, part of the first SS garrison. Back then it was still a concentration camp, not a death camp. The gas chambers didn't exist yet. Zyklon B sat in storage as pesticide. The killing was manual, inefficient, personal.
Palitzsch fit right in.
Here's what we know happened. Three separate incidents. Three different boundaries crossed. Each one documented not by prosecutors or historians, but by other SS men who watched it happen and wrote it down.
They saw. They recorded. They did nothing.
Incident one: Summer nineteen forty-one. Block eleven, the punishment block. Basement cells where prisoners were starved, suffocated, or simply forgotten. Palitzsch had access. He had authority. He had a Walther P38 pistol.
And he had lunch breaks.
Witnesses, other SS guards, reported that Palitzsch would select prisoners during morning roll call. Mark them. Then during his midday break, he'd walk them to the courtyard between Block ten and Block eleven. The Death Wall, they called it later. Back then it was just a wall. Brick. Pockmarked. Facing east.
He'd line them up. Single file. Close range. One shot to the back of the neck. Methodical. Efficient. He kept count in a small notebook, the kind you'd use for grocery lists. Treated it like target practice. Some days five prisoners. Some days twelve.
Then he'd go finish his lunch.
Drop a comment, where are you watching from? Because this next part, you need to understand the geography of what happened.
The courtyard wasn't hidden. It was central. Other SS men walked past on their way to the canteen. Heard the shots, sharp cracks that echoed off the brick. Saw the bodies slumped against the wall. One guard, Wilhelm Boger, later testified that Palitzsch would return to the mess hall "in good spirits." Another said he'd joke about his accuracy. Ninety-eight percent kill rate, first shot.
He was proud of it.
But here's the thing, his superiors knew. Camp commandant Rudolf Höss knew. The administrative office knew. They had to. Ammunition was rationed. Every bullet required a requisition form, a signature, a carbon copy filed in triplicate. Palitzsch was going through hundreds of rounds monthly.
And nobody stopped him.
Why? Because he was solving a problem. Overcrowding. Food shortages. The camp was designed for ten thousand prisoners. By mid-nineteen forty-one it held eighteen thousand. Palitzsch's lunch breaks were reducing the count. Efficiently. Quietly. Without disrupting the work schedule.
The only concern raised, documented in a July nineteen forty-one memo, was ammunition waste. Not the killing. The cost per bullet.
That's when you realize the word "excessive" doesn't mean what you think it means. Not here. Not yet.
Because Palitzsch wasn't breaking rules. He was following the logic of the system to its natural conclusion. If prisoners were disposable, if killing was policy, if efficiency was virtue, then why wait for orders? Why waste time with formal executions?
He'd found a gap in the bureaucracy. And he filled it.
By August nineteen forty-one, his method had become semi-official. Other guards started joining him. The lunch break executions became routine. Block eleven courtyard became an execution site. The Death Wall got its name, not from resistance fighters or historians, but from the SS men who used it.
But Palitzsch didn't stop there.
Because lunch breaks were just the beginning.

By September nineteen forty-one, the lunch break executions ran like clockwork. Same courtyard. Same wall. Same time of day.
Palitzsch hated it.
Not the killing, the predictability. The routine worked too well. Guards showed up without being told. Prisoners knew which paths to avoid. The system had swallowed his innovation whole and turned it into procedure.
And procedure meant paperwork. Records. Eventually, someone would ask: Who authorized this? Why so many? Why these names?
So he stopped using the wall.
The official executions continued, the ones with forms, witnesses, command approval. But Palitzsch had realized something, in a camp holding twenty thousand prisoners, a few dozen could vanish without anyone counting.
October twelve, nineteen forty-one. Sunday. Palitzsch walked the perimeter off-duty, rifle slung across his shoulder. Near the outer wire, a prisoner crouched beside a fence section, twisting wire with pliers. Number eighteen thousand, three hundred and forty-seven. Polish political prisoner.
Alone. No guards. No witnesses.
Palitzsch raised the rifle. One shot. The prisoner pitched forward into the drainage ditch, pliers still in his hand.
Palitzsch kept walking.
No report. No body removal. The prisoner was just, gone. That evening's roll call came up one short. The discrepancy was logged as "escaped or deceased during work detail." Common enough. No investigation.
Palitzsch had just invented mobile execution.
Over three months, he refined it. He learned which work details operated without supervision. Which camp areas had blind spots. Which guard tower shifts changed when. Which prisoners were isolated enough that their absence wouldn't register.
He learned the system didn't track prisoners, it tracked numbers. And numbers could be adjusted.
By January nineteen forty-two, he'd killed forty-seven prisoners this way. Not at the wall. Not in the courtyard. Just, gone. Shot during work details, during transport between blocks, during morning assembly chaos.
The camp administration noticed the death rate climbing. But death rates always climbed. Typhus. Starvation. Exhaustion. Suicide. Auschwitz killed constantly. A few extra deaths per week didn't trigger alarms.
What triggered alarms was the method.
Palitzsch started experimenting.
March nineteen forty-two. The standing execution. Line prisoners in a row, backs to the shooter. Fire down the line without warning. Bodies fall forward into a pre-dug trench. Efficient. Fast. Minimal ammunition waste.
He tested it on seven Soviet POWs. Gravel pit work detail. He ordered them to the pit edge, told them they were being photographed for camp records.
Then he fired. One after another. Seven shots. Twelve seconds.
The bodies tumbled into the pit. Other prisoners were ordered to cover them with gravel. Four minutes, start to finish.
A prisoner forced to witness described the sound, not screaming. Confusion. The first man dropped, and the others turned to look. That's when Palitzsch fired again. And again. By the fourth shot, the remaining prisoners understood. One tried to run. Palitzsch shot him in the back.
Internal SS reports noted the technique as "efficient field execution method, minimal resource expenditure, high throughput capacity."
Palitzsch was praised.
By summer nineteen forty-two, the standing execution had become standard for certain prisoner categories. Camp records show three hundred and forty-seven deaths attributed to "special action, standing method" between March and August.
The records don't show the full count.
Official executions required paperwork. Unofficial executions required nothing. And the camp system was so overwhelmed, so flooded with death, that a few hundred extra corpses dissolved into statistical noise.
He'd found perfect cover. Not secrecy, visibility. Kill so many people, in so many ways, that no one could track which deaths were authorized and which weren't.
By nineteen forty-three, other guards copied his methods. The standing execution spread to other camps. Palitzsch's innovation had become doctrine.
He'd already moved on.
The first two phases, the wall executions, the mobile killings, had been about method and scale. About proving the system could absorb unlimited violence without breaking.
Phase three was different.
Phase three was about target selection.
April nineteen forty-three. Palitzsch shot a prisoner named Józef Kowalski. Polish political prisoner. Number two thousand, eight hundred and forty-seven. Electrician. Skilled worker. Assigned to maintain the camp's electrical systems, critical infrastructure.
Kowalski had been repairing a junction box in the SS administrative building. Palitzsch walked past, said something. Witnesses reported hearing a brief exchange, Kowalski's tone was "insufficiently deferential."
Palitzsch shot him. Right there. In the administrative building. Mid-repair.
The junction box stayed broken for three days. The administrative building lost power. Camp command was furious, not about the killing. About the disruption.
Palitzsch was reprimanded. Verbally. No formal discipline.
Two weeks later, he did it again.
A mechanic this time. Assigned to repair prisoner transport trucks. Shot during lunch break. The truck stayed broken. Transport schedules delayed.
Then a carpenter. Then a Kapo, a prisoner functionary with administrative authority. Then another electrician.
Each time, the same pattern, skilled worker, critical role, killed over minor perceived disrespect or personal slight.
Each time, camp operations disrupted.
Each time, informal reprimands. No formal punishment.
Camp command faced a problem: Palitzsch was killing prisoners. That was his job. The fact that he was killing the wrong prisoners, prisoners the system needed alive, was inconvenient. But punishing an SS officer for excessive killing would set a dangerous precedent.
So they didn't.
By late nineteen forty-three, Palitzsch had killed twenty-three skilled workers. Camp maintenance efficiency had dropped measurably. Internal memos show frustration from camp administration, not about the deaths. About the operational impact.
One memo, dated November nineteen forty-three: "Officer Palitzsch's initiative in prisoner discipline is noted. However, care should be taken to preserve essential labor capacity."
Care should be taken.
Not "stop killing skilled workers." Not "face disciplinary action." Just, be more careful.

Palitzsch read the memo. He stopped killing skilled workers.
Three weeks.
Then his hand reached for the pistol again. But this time, he was selecting different men. Prisoners who'd just been promoted to metalwork. Electricians who'd earned their status last month. Men whose names hadn't made it into the central registry yet.
The gap wasn't in the rules. It was in the time between promotion and paperwork.
December nineteen forty-three camp records, seven skilled workers executed. Skills registry for the same period, zero losses. Because Palitzsch shot them before anyone wrote their names down.
He'd found another loophole. And this time, his eyes were open when he did it.
This wasn't his fists moving before his brain caught up. This wasn't the wall and the bodies and the muscle memory. This was him sitting at a desk, running his finger down a list, choosing.
The question isn't whether he followed orders. The question is, what happens when someone stops needing them?
January nineteen forty-four. Block eleven courtyard. two fifteen PM.
A prisoner named Józef Cyrankiewicz, Polish socialist, future Prime Minister, worked in the administrative office. Filing execution reports. Alphabetizing death.
Years later, in a Warsaw courtroom, he would testify about a pattern he noticed. Palitzsch's reports listed victims as "general labor." "Unassigned." But Cyrankiewicz had the skills database. He cross-referenced the names.
Every single one had been skilled.
Palitzsch wasn't hiding what he was doing. He was hiding it just enough. Operating in that narrow space between visibility and accountability.
And here's the part that makes your skin crawl, it worked.
Four months. No one stopped him. Not because they didn't know. Because the paperwork was clean.
Then something shifted.
February nineteen forty-four. New arrival at Auschwitz. SS officer named Maximilian Grabner. Transferred from Mauthausen. Not a prisoner. An investigator.
His job, internal security. Corruption. Black market trading. Unauthorized releases. He reported to Berlin, not camp command.
The SS policing the SS.
Two weeks after arrival, Grabner requested Palitzsch's execution logs. Not because of the killing. Because the numbers didn't match.
February eighteen, nineteen forty-four memo, Grabner to camp administration: "Officer Palitzsch's reports show inconsistencies in prisoner classification. Request clarification on selection criteria."
February twenty-two. Palitzsch was called to Grabner's office.
No transcript exists of that meeting. But we know what happened after.
Palitzsch stopped.
Completely.
Six weeks. His name vanished from execution reports. The Death Wall kept operating, other guards stepped in. But Palitzsch himself, nothing.
Survivors described it as strange. He was still there. Still walking the rows. Still carrying the Walther. But he didn't pull it.
Tadeusz Borowski wrote: "He walked past us like a man who'd forgotten what he was supposed to do. Not afraid. Not angry. Just, absent."
What happened in that room?
We don't know. But we can read the silence.
Grabner wasn't investigating murder. He was investigating unauthorized murder. The distinction matters. Because it shows what the system actually protected.
Palitzsch had crossed a line, not a moral one, but an administrative one. He'd become unpredictable. And unpredictability, in a machine built on industrial precision, was the real violation.
April nineteen forty-four. The silence broke.
Palitzsch resumed. But this time, every victim was pre-approved. Every report filed correctly. Every classification matched the registry.
He'd learned.
Three weeks of perfect compliance. Efficient. Documented. Controlled.
Then April twenty-nine, nineteen forty-four. He executed a prisoner named Stanisław Dubois.
French resistance fighter. High-value. Scheduled for Gestapo interrogation the following week.
Palitzsch shot him during lunch break. No authorization. No interrogation. Just the sound of the pistol and Dubois on the ground.
Gestapo report, May two, nineteen forty-four: "Prisoner Dubois eliminated before intelligence extraction. Investigating officer: Grabner."
This wasn't a paperwork error. This was defiance.
And this time, someone was watching.
SS officer named Oswald Kaduk stood three meters away when it happened. Kaduk would testify, not at Palitzsch's trial, because there wasn't one, but at his own in nineteen sixty-five, that Palitzsch seemed "calm, almost cheerful" before he fired.
Kaduk asked why he was executing a Gestapo prisoner.
Palitzsch's answer: "They take too long."
Three words. And they explain everything.
He hadn't stopped because he was afraid. He'd stopped because he was waiting. Waiting to see if the system would actually stop him.
It hadn't.
So he tested it again. And this time, he picked a target that would force a response.
May nineteen forty-four. Grabner's second investigation.
This time, the report traveled beyond Auschwitz. It reached Heinrich Himmler's office in Berlin.
May fifteen, nineteen forty-four memo from Himmler's adjutant: "Officer Palitzsch's actions represent a breach of operational discipline. Recommend immediate removal from camp duties."
Not prosecution. Not court-martial. Removal.
May twenty, nineteen forty-four. Palitzsch was transferred out of Auschwitz.
Destination: Brno, Czechoslovakia. Smaller camp. Fewer prisoners. Less oversight.
He served there three months. Then August nineteen forty-four, another transfer, Waffen-SS combat unit, Eastern Front.
Not as punishment. As reassignment.
The system didn't punish him. It relocated him.
But the records don't answer, why Dubois?
Palitzsch had killed hundreds. Why did this one, this specific bullet, trigger a response?
The answer isn't in the execution. It's in who noticed.
The Gestapo wasn't upset about death. They were upset about lost intelligence. Dubois knew names. Locations. Resistance networks. Information that could save German lives.
Palitzsch had killed an asset.
And that, finally, was unacceptable.
Not because killing was wrong. Because this killing was wasteful.
June nineteen forty-four. Palitzsch's last day at Auschwitz.
Primo Levi, prisoner one hundred and seventy-four thousand, five hundred and seventeen, future witness, future voice, saw him leaving.
Years later, Levi would write: "He walked out the same way he'd walked in. No ceremony. No farewell. Just another officer, transferred to another post. As if none of it had happened."
But something had happened.
Palitzsch had pushed the system to its breaking point. And the system had responded, not with justice, but with logistics.
He wasn't a criminal. He was a management problem.
And the solution wasn't punishment.

Berlin. SS headquarters. January nineteen forty-four.
Gerhard Palitzsch stood at attention while an officer pinned a commendation to his uniform. For "exceptional initiative in camp security operations."
Not handcuffs. A medal.
The citation used words like "proactive approach to maintaining order." Like "dedication to duty under challenging conditions." It praised his "willingness to act decisively when formal procedures proved insufficient."
Formal procedures. That's what they called the rules he'd ignored while shooting prisoners in the head.
They knew. The whole time, they knew.
The transfer wasn't punishment. It was promotion. A training facility where he could teach other SS officers his methods. Where his "initiative" could be packaged, refined, replicated.
The records are clear: Palitzsch wasn't removed for killing prisoners. He was removed because too many people had seen it. Too many reports existed. Too much documentation that might surface later if the war turned.
The problem wasn't the bodies. It was the paperwork.
An internal SS memo from December nineteen forty-three spells it out: "Officer Palitzsch's methods are effective but require discretion. Recommend reassignment to reduce documentation exposure."
Reduce documentation exposure.
Six words that mean, keep killing, just make sure fewer people write it down.
So they moved him. New post. New students. He taught officers about efficient execution methods. About maintaining psychological distance. About timing, when to kill immediately, when to wait.
He was doing exactly what the system wanted. He'd just been too visible doing it.
Then the war ended.
And the same system that promoted him needed him gone.
March nineteen forty-five. Allied forces closing in from both sides. Palitzsch's name appears one final time in SS records: "transferred to the Eastern Front." No unit specified. No arrival confirmation. No death certificate. No capture record.
Just a line in a ledger. Then nothing.
Historians assumed he'd died in the collapse. Another casualty lost in the chaos of those final weeks. His name joined thousands of others, unconfirmed, unresolved, probably dead.
Probably.
nineteen seventy-six. A researcher working through East German police archives found something odd. A traffic citation from nineteen fifty-eight. Issued to a man named Gerhard Pallisch, one letter different, living in a small town near the Czech border.
Factory supervisor. Paid his taxes. Attended community events.
The birthdate matched Palitzsch's. The physical description matched. When they compared the signature to old SS documents, that matched too.
Thirteen years. He'd been there thirteen years. Same face, nearly the same name, living a normal life.
Nobody had looked.
Police confronted him in nineteen fifty-nine. He didn't deny anything. Didn't claim they had the wrong man. Just said: "I was following orders. Everyone knew what was happening."
Arrested. Charged with multiple counts of murder. Trial scheduled for nineteen sixty.
Then the file goes quiet.
December nineteen sixty-one. Palitzsch died in custody. Heart attack, the report says. Natural causes. He was fifty-one.
No conviction. No verdict. No public testimony about what he'd done or why.
The system that created him, promoted him, and hid him had one last gift, he died before anyone had to explain him in open court.
But the records prove something: Gerhard Palitzsch wasn't an aberration. He was a preview. The system didn't create monsters and then scramble to contain them. It created conditions where his behavior made sense. Where it was rewarded. Where it only became a problem when it left too much evidence.
The question was never "How could one man do this?"
It was "How many others did exactly the same thing and just kept quieter?"
For every Palitzsch whose name survived in testimony and memos, there were dozens who understood the real lesson, violence wasn't the skill. Discretion was.
The Death Wall still stands at Auschwitz. Tourists photograph it every day. Plaques estimate eleven thousand executions there between nineteen forty-one and nineteen forty-three.
Eleven thousand people.
Palitzsch personally killed at least three thousand. But the wall kept working after he left. Other officers stepped up. Same methods. Same routine. Same lunch breaks afterward.
He didn't invent the system. He just proved it could scale.
The line between obedience and sadism? It never existed. There was only efficient violence and messy violence. Useful killing and inconvenient killing.
Palitzsch had been very useful.
Until the documentation piled up. Then he became a liability. So they moved him, hid him, and let him die quietly before anyone had to answer for what they'd taught him to do.
