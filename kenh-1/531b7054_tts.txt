The Netflix profile was called 'Me and Baby' with a due date in the calendar, and I was still logged into his account from three months ago when we watched movies in bed.
The profile picture was a sonogram. Gray and white static with a tiny bean shape in the center.
I wasn't pregnant.
My hands started trembling. I had to set the laptop on my passenger seat before I dropped it. The parking lot lights were coming on even though it was only four-thirty. November in Ohio. Everything gets dark too early.
I was parked outside Target because I couldn't go home yet. Couldn't walk back into that apartment where his side of the closet was still half-full of hangers. Where the bathroom counter still had that ring from his shaving cream. Six weeks since he said he needed space and he still hadn't come back for the rest of his things.
Space.
The profile had been created four days ago. I checked the date twice. Which meant he'd moved on before he even finished moving out.
I picked up my phone. My thumb hovered over his name for a second before I scrolled past it. Texted Sarah instead: "I lost him to someone pregnant."
Three dots appeared immediately. Then disappeared. Then appeared again.
Her response came through: "That's rough. Can't talk rn, at dinner with Jake's parents."
I read it three times. No call. No "omg are you okay." No "I'm coming over." Just five words and back to her perfect relationship with her perfect fiancé and his parents who probably loved her.
I sat there for twenty minutes watching people push carts through the sliding doors. A mom with two kids hanging off the sides. An old couple walking so slowly they were barely moving. A teenage girl on her phone. Normal people buying normal things. Laundry detergent. Throw pillows. A life.
This was Sunday. The Netflix thing.
But the unraveling had started that morning.
I'd gone to church. Our church, the one we'd attended together for two years, where everyone knew us as a couple, where we'd volunteered for the youth group fundraiser last spring. I thought maybe being around people who knew me would help. That someone would ask how I was doing and I could say "not great" and they'd sit with me or pray with me or something.
I saw Monica first. She was standing by the coffee station in that same floral dress she wore every other week. We usually talked there before service, complained about work, made plans to grab lunch. I walked toward her and watched her eyes land on me for half a second, then snap away like she'd touched something hot. She turned her whole body and started talking to someone else.
Okay. Maybe she didn't see me.
But then it happened again with David and his wife in the hallway. They were walking straight toward me and I smiled, this small, hopeful smile that felt pathetic even as I was doing it, and David put his hand on his wife's back and steered her in the other direction. Like I was a puddle they were avoiding.
Drop a comment if you've ever felt invisible in a room full of people who are supposed to care about you.
I found a seat in the back. Alone. The row in front of me filled up with a family of five. The row behind me filled up with college kids. The seats on either side of me stayed empty even as people squeezed into the middle sections.
During the greeting time when everyone shakes hands and hugs, the couple in front of me turned to the people beside them. The dad high-fived his kids. Never looked back at me once.
I left before the sermon ended. Grabbed my purse and walked out while the pastor was mid-sentence about forgiveness or grace or something. Sat in my car in the church parking lot crying like some kind of cliché. Mascara running. Nose running. The whole thing.
That's when I saw my sister.
She was walking to her car with her husband and their two kids. The kids were fighting over a donut. Normal Sunday morning chaos. I rolled down my window. Started to call out to her.
She saw me. We made eye contact. I know she saw me.
Then she grabbed her husband's arm and they walked faster. She actually sped up.
My own sister.
I drove home in this fog. Couldn't process what was happening. Why everyone was treating me like I'd done something wrong when I was the one who'd been left. I was the one sleeping on an air mattress in the living room because he'd taken the bed. I was the one who had to explain to our landlord why I needed to break the lease two months early.
I tried calling my mom. Straight to voicemail. Her voice bright and cheerful: "You've reached Linda, leave a message!"
Tried Sarah again. It rang six times. No answer.
So I did what you do when you're alone and you can't stand the silence anymore. I opened my laptop to watch something mindless. Something that would let me stop thinking for an hour.
That's when I saw it.
I'd forgotten I was still logged into his Netflix account. We'd shared it for so long it was automatic, I'd type N-E and my fingers would fill in the rest. And there it was, right at the top of the profile list, between his main profile and the one we'd made for my niece.
'Me and Baby.'
I clicked on it before I could stop myself. The viewing history loaded. Pregnancy documentaries. Birth vlogs. Something called "Preparing for Fatherhood." All watched in the last four days.
The due date in the calendar was seven months away.
Which meant she was already two months along.
Which meant he'd gotten someone pregnant while we were still together. While we were still sharing a bed. While I was still doing his laundry.
I sat there on my air mattress in my half-empty apartment, staring at that sonogram profile picture, and something clicked into place. Like a door closing.
The way everyone had looked at me at church. The avoidance. The speed-walking. My sister pretending she didn't see me.
They knew.
They all knew something I didn't.
And whatever it was, they'd already decided I was the villain in this story.

I couldn't get a full breath for three days.
Not the kind where you're doubled over. The kind where your lungs just quit halfway through and you're left with this shallow sip of air that doesn't go anywhere.
I kept pulling up Facebook. Closing the app. Opening it thirty seconds later.
Her profile was wide open. Public. She was the type who posted scripture over stock photos of beaches and checked in at every church potluck with "Grateful for this community ❤️."
Bethany.
I didn't know her. Not beyond the polite nod in the lobby. She was in the young marrieds group, the one I'd quietly stopped being invited to after I turned thirty-two without a ring. They met Friday nights for Settlers of Catan and inside jokes I'd never be part of.
But I'd seen her. Blonde hair always in those perfect loose waves. Smile like a door closing softly in your face.
She'd been at the church picnic last summer. I remembered the charcuterie board. Prosciutto roses. Three kinds of cheese. Everyone circled around it saying how talented she was, how much effort that must've taken. I'd brought Chips Ahoy on a paper plate.
I scrolled back through her timeline.
Fourth of July. Group photo at someone's deck party. There, in the back, half out of frame. Him. My ex. Red solo cup. Navy shirt I'd wrapped in tissue paper for his birthday.
June. Hiking trail photo. "His creation is unmatched 🙌." In the comments, buried under a dozen flame emojis, his name. "Good catching up yesterday."
Yesterday. That would've been Sunday.
The Sunday I'd texted asking if he wanted to meet for coffee. The Sunday he'd said he was "slammed with family stuff."
I screenshotted it. My thumb shaking slightly. Like I was collecting evidence for a court that didn't exist.
My phone lit up. My sister.
"Hey, can we talk?"
I stared at the message. Four words that felt like twenty.
We hadn't really talked since the breakup. A few "thinking of you 💕" texts that could've been sent to anyone. She'd liked one Instagram post in three months. A sunset. Safe. Nothing that required follow-up.
I typed: "Sure."
Three dots. Gone. Back again.
Then she called.
I let it ring twice before I answered.
"Hey." Her voice had that hostage negotiator softness.
The silence had weight.
"So." She cleared her throat. "I heard you've been. I heard you saw something. Online."
My stomach dropped through the floor. "How did you, "
"People talk." Fast. Apologetic. "I just wanted to check in."
"You don't sound fine."
I wasn't fine. I was sitting in the dark at two in the afternoon because turning on the lights felt like too much effort. I'd eaten Frosted Flakes for dinner three nights running. I'd stopped getting my mail because every piece of junk addressed to "Current Resident" felt like a personal attack.
But I said: "I'm fine."
Silence again.
"Look." She exhaled hard. "I know this is awful. But maybe. maybe it's better you found out now. Before someone told you at church or something."
"Everyone at church already knew." It came out like a slap.
"That's not, "
"You knew. Didn't you."
She didn't answer. The pause was a confession.
"When?" I asked.
"It's not, "
"A few weeks ago. Maybe a month. I don't, "
"I didn't know how to tell you. I didn't want to make it worse."
I laughed. It sounded like something breaking. "So you just let me walk around like an idiot instead."
"That's not fair."
"What's not fair is everyone at church knowing my ex got someone pregnant and nobody thought I should maybe hear about it. What's not fair is finding out on Facebook like I'm some, "
"She's not just someone."
I stopped breathing.
My sister's voice dropped. "Bethany. She's not just. they didn't just hook up. They're together. Like, actually together."
The walls tilted.
"They're engaged," she said. "He proposed last week."
My hands went numb.
"The baby's due in November. They're getting married in October. At the church. Pastor Mike's officiating."
Pastor Mike. Who'd met with us six times for premarital counseling. Who'd said we were "really solid." Who'd prayed over our future like it was a sure thing.
"I'm sorry," my sister said. "I thought you should know before, "
"Before the announcement. They're doing it this Sunday. During service. He's going to share their testimony."
Their testimony.
Like this was a Hallmark movie. Like God had orchestrated it. Like I was just a commercial break between his origin story and his happy ending.
"I have to go," I said.
I hung up.
My hands were shaking hard now. I opened Facebook again.
Bethany's profile. Her photos. Her friends.
And there, pinned at the top: "In a relationship with, "
His name. His face. The two of them, her hand flat on his chest, diamond catching the camera flash.
Posted six days ago.
Sixty-three likes. Forty-two comments. "Congratulations!" "So happy for you guys!" "God is SO good!"
I scrolled to the comments.
And there it was.
My sister's name. Posted five days ago.
One red heart.
I stared at that heart until my eyes burned.
Then I clicked his profile.
And saw the post. From this morning. The one I'd somehow missed.
Photo of them. Her in a white sundress that wasn't a wedding dress but wasn't not a wedding dress. Him in the blue button-down I'd ironed so many times I knew which collar point always flipped wrong.
Caption: "Can't wait to share our story this Sunday. God's timing is perfect. number Blessed number NewBeginnings"
Someone in the comments: "How did you two meet?"
His reply: "Funny story actually. We'll share more on Sunday, but let's just say God works in mysterious ways. Sometimes the person you're meant to be with is right in front of you, you just have to be brave enough to see it."
I read it three times.
Right in front of you.
I opened my camera roll. Scrolled back. Back to last summer. The picnic.
There. The group photo someone had posted in the church Facebook group.
Me on the left. Him in the middle.
Bethany on the right. Looking at him. Not the camera.
Him.

Her hand was on his arm.

That day. The picnic. He'd been off. Quiet. I'd asked what was wrong and he'd said he was "just tired." We'd left early. He said he had a headache.
