TITLE: In 1959, we detonated nuclear bombs to test a spacecraft. It worked.

=== PART 1 ===

A steel plate, six storeys wide, polished to a mirror finish on one side and scarred black on the other. Behind it, a ship large enough to carry families. Beneath it, a nuclear fireball blooming every second. This was not science fiction. This was Project Orion — and it had a budget. The central question is straightforward, and it deserves a direct answer: Could humanity have reached the outer planets by 1970 — not through speculation, but through engineering that already existed? The evidence suggests yes. What follows is the story of why we didn't. [THE BOMB MAKER'S MEMO]

In 1946, a Polish-born mathematician named Stanislaw Ulam proposed something that should have changed the trajectory of human spaceflight. He had spent the war years at Los Alamos, calculating yields for atomic weapons. Now he turned those same calculations in a different direction. Not toward destruction. Toward propulsion. By 1947, Ulam and physicist Frederick Reines had completed preliminary calculations in a Los Alamos memorandum. The concept was blunt: detonate nuclear bombs behind a spacecraft, and ride the blast wave forward. No elegant chemistry. No refined combustion chambers. Raw nuclear force, directed and repeated. The idea sat in classified files for nearly a decade. Then, in August 1955, Ulam co-authored a paper with Cornelius Everett that formalized the approach — fission bombs "ejected and detonated at a considerable distance" from the vehicle, followed by disks of solid propellant to shape the plasma into usable thrust. This was not a thought experiment. These were men who had built the bombs in question. They understood precisely what those weapons could do if pointed the right way. Consider the timeline. The first artificial satellite, Sputnik, would not launch until October 1957. Ulam's propulsion concept predated the Space Age itself by over a decade. Let that settle for a moment. [THE QUIET ENGINEER]

In July 1958, ARPA — the agency that would become DARPA — agreed to fund the program. The work would be based at General Atomics in San Diego, a company founded by Frederick de Hoffman to develop commercial nuclear reactors. But the driving force behind Orion was not a reactor designer. Ted Taylor was a mild-mannered Los Alamos physicist. He held no PhD. What he held instead was a record unmatched in the American weapons program: he had designed the largest, the smallest, and the most efficient fission bombs in the U.S. arsenal. His specialty was not making bigger explosions. It was making precise ones — shaping nuclear detonations so that bomb debris would, as one account put it, "squirt in one particular direction."

This distinction matters. Taylor was not repurposing abstract theory. He was repurposing proven weapons engineering — redirecting the focused violence of nuclear shaped charges into controlled, repeatable thrust. De Hoffman recruited Freeman Dyson from the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton to provide the theoretical framework. Dyson, one of the most respected physicists of his generation, arrived in San Diego in 1958. The project's internal motto, according to Dyson himself, was direct enough: "Mars by 1965, Saturn by 1970."

That was not aspiration dressed as poetry. If you examine the performance calculations — specific impulse values that exceeded chemical rockets by orders of magnitude, transit times measured in weeks rather than years — the motto reads differently. It was an engineering schedule. A funded one, with staff, prototypes, and a classified design office working through the math. By the late 1950s, the team at General Atomics had moved beyond paper. Real calculations yielded real blueprints. Blueprints demanded tests. And they built them. Not full-scale nuclear tests — not yet. Working models, designed to validate the core principle: that a plate could survive repeated explosive impacts and translate them into forward motion. What happened next proved the concept was sound. It also proved something far more uncomfortable — about what riding nuclear explosions would actually cost. === PART 2 ===

[THE ANATOMY OF CONTROLLED APOCALYPSE]

How do you ride a nuclear explosion without vaporizing the ship? The answer is not elegant. It does not involve deflection, or avoidance, or any of the graceful solutions that chemical rocketry would later pursue. The answer is blunt: you place a slab of steel between the bomb and the crew, and you let the blast hit it. Everything about Project Orion's propulsion system follows from that single, brutal premise. Each nuclear charge was small — deliberately so. Ted Taylor had spent years at Los Alamos perfecting the art of miniaturization, designing fission devices compact enough to fit inside an artillery shell. For Orion, he applied that expertise in reverse: rather than maximizing destructive yield, he shaped each charge to direct its energy in a single direction. The technical term is "shaped nuclear charge." The practical meaning is that when the bomb detonated, the resulting plasma — superheated gas expanding at thousands of kilometres per second — would not radiate uniformly in all directions. It would squirt. Downward, and outward, in a focused cone aimed directly at the pusher plate. That plate was the heart of the system. Six storeys in diameter. Forged from high-strength steel, its underside coated with a thin layer of ablative material designed to vaporize on contact with plasma and reform between detonations. The topside — the side facing the ship — remained pristine. Mirror-polished. Almost decorative, given what was happening beneath it. When the plasma struck, momentum transferred. The plate lurched upward. One pulse. One jolt. A single nuclear detonation producing a brief, violent spike of acceleration. This is where the engineering becomes remarkable. That spike — measured in tens of thousands of G's at the plate surface — would kill every living thing aboard if transmitted directly to the crew compartment. The solution was not to reduce the force. It was to stretch it across time. Connecting the pusher plate to the ship's main body were massive pneumatic shock absorbers — hydraulic pistons with a stroke length of approximately eight metres, roughly the height of a three-storey building, compressing and extending with each detonation to absorb forces that would otherwise be instantly lethal. Their function was identical to the suspension on an automobile, scaled up by a factor that defies casual analogy. Each detonation compressed the pistons. The pistons extended slowly, transferring the instantaneous impulse into a sustained, survivable push lasting several seconds. By the time the acceleration reached the crew deck, the G-forces had been smoothed to between two and four G's. Uncomfortable. Sustained. But survivable. One detonation per second. For minutes, then hours, then days. Thousands of nuclear charges ejected from a magazine in the ship's core, fed through a hole in the centre of the pusher plate, flung thirty metres behind the vessel, and detonated in sequence. The rhythm would have been metronomic. A flash. A jolt. A flash. A jolt. Each one pushing the ship fractionally faster. [THE BOMB MAKER'S PRECISION]

What made this conceivable — what separated it from absurdity — was Taylor's specific expertise. At Los Alamos, he had designed the smallest fission device ever tested by the United States. He understood, with empirical precision, how to control the geometry of a nuclear explosion. The charges for Orion were not standard warheads repurposed for propulsion. They were purpose-built pulse units: compact, shaped, and tuned to maximise momentum transfer while minimising residual radiation at the plate surface. This distinction mattered enormously. A standard nuclear detonation releases energy as roughly equal parts thermal radiation, kinetic blast, and ionizing radiation. Taylor's shaped charges redirected the energy budget — concentrating kinetic output toward the plate; shielding against penetrating gamma rays and neutrons remained a separate, mass-intensive engineering requirement. Shielding would still be required. Metres of polyethylene for neutron absorption — in some configurations, walls thicker than a standard interior door is wide — and layers of tungsten for gamma attenuation, a metal so dense that a cube thirty centimetres on each side weighs more than 170 kilograms. That shielding added mass — in some designs, nearly fifty percent of the total ship weight, meaning a 4,000-tonne vessel would carry roughly 2,000 tonnes of shielding material alone, equivalent to the fully loaded displacement of a World War II destroyer dedicated entirely to radiation protection. Mass, however, was not the constraint it is for chemical rockets. This is the central paradox of nuclear pulse propulsion: the heavier the ship, the better it works. A larger vessel absorbs more momentum per pulse, smooths acceleration more effectively, and carries more shielding without proportional penalty. Chemical rockets obey the tyranny of the rocket equation, where every additional kilogram of payload demands exponentially more fuel. Orion inverted that logic. Payload capacity scaled favourably with size. The specific impulse — the measure of propulsive efficiency — told the story in a single number. Chemical rockets of the era achieved specific impulses between 300 and 450 seconds. Orion's design calculations yielded values between 6,000 seconds for the fission-pulse variant and up to 100,000 seconds for designs incorporating thermonuclear charges, depending on the pulse unit configuration. By contrast, even modern ion thrusters rarely exceed 10,000 seconds — and they produce thrust measurable in millinewtons. Orion produced thrust measurable in meganewtons. What followed from these numbers were mission profiles that read like fiction but were grounded in verified physics. A Mars transit of four weeks, not nine months. A crewed mission to Saturn carrying 150 personnel, with payload capacity for scientific equipment, habitation modules, and return fuel. Ships massing 4,000 tonnes at launch — comparable to the displacement of a World War II-era Gleaves-class destroyer at approximately 2,395 tonnes full load, and roughly eight times the launch mass of the Saturn V at 2,970 tonnes. The proposed Mars variant alone called for an eight-person crew, 125 tonnes of payload, and roughly 800 pulse units stored in an internal magazine. During powered flight, the ship would have been visible from Earth's surface — a new point of light in the sky, pulsing once per second with the brightness of a small sun. Launch would have registered on seismographs worldwide. This was not subtle technology. It was not meant to be. In 1959, the team validated the core principle with conventional explosives. A scale model — a steel plate suspended above shaped chemical charges — was detonated repeatedly at Point Loma, near San Diego. The plate survived. It flew. The footage, still preserved, shows a small metal disc rising smoothly on a column of sequential blasts, stable and controlled. The concept worked. By 1962, however, diplomats in Geneva and Moscow were negotiating something that no amount of engineering could overcome. The atmospheric test ban discussions had entered their final phase. Fallout projections for an Orion ground launch were being calculated — not in tonnes of thrust, but in excess cancer deaths per mission. The numbers were not speculative. They were statistical, peer-reviewed, and—

=== PART 3 ===

—undeniable. [THE PRICE OF EVERY DETONATION]

On August 5, 1963, representatives from the United States, the Soviet Union, and the United Kingdom signed the Partial Nuclear Test Ban Treaty in Moscow. Nuclear weapon tests in the atmosphere, in outer space, and underwater were now prohibited. The agreement entered into force on October 10. For the diplomats, this was arms control. For the engineers in San Diego, it was a death sentence delivered in triplicate. The arithmetic had been clear from the beginning. Each nuclear pulse unit in the Orion design carried a yield of approximately 0.15 kilotons — small, by weapons standards. Roughly one percent of the Hiroshima bomb. A mission to Mars would require approximately 800 such detonations. Saturn demanded several thousand. Every single pulse, if fired within Earth's atmosphere, would release radioactive isotopes into the stratosphere: strontium-90, which accumulates in bone; cesium-137, which disperses through soil and water systems. The fallout would not remain local. Stratospheric winds distribute globally. Across national borders. Across oceans. Across populations that had never consented to the risk. The Orion team calculated the cost. Their own estimates suggested between one and ten statistical fatalities per launch — excess cancer deaths distributed invisibly across the world's population, untraceable to any single individual but measurable in aggregate. This was not an engineering flaw. The propulsion system's exhaust was nuclear fallout, and no amount of plate shaping or blast optimization could change the fundamental chemistry of fission products. No nation would accept another country detonating nuclear weapons in shared airspace, regardless of the stated purpose. The geopolitical implications were as immovable as the physics were sound. What followed was not a dramatic confrontation. There was no moment where Ted Taylor stood before a committee and argued for the stars. The treaty simply made the primary launch profile — ground-to-orbit under nuclear pulse power — illegal under international law. Negotiations had been underway since 1958, the same year DARPA began funding Orion. The diplomatic world and the engineering world had been on a collision course from the beginning. They reached it in Moscow, with three signatures. [DESIGNS FOR A WORLD THAT NEVER CAME]

By the time the treaty was signed, the Orion team had not merely validated a concept. They had produced detailed engineering blueprints for interplanetary spacecraft of a kind that has never been matched since. The largest design variant massed 4,000 tonnes — heavier than a naval destroyer. Its habitable sections included rotating crew modules to simulate gravity, multi-deck laboratories, cargo bays sufficient for years of supplies. In certain configurations: botanical gardens intended for atmospheric recycling on voyages lasting months or years. These were not conceptual sketches produced for funding presentations. They were engineering documents with mass budgets calculated to the kilogram, radiation shielding specifications measured in centimeters of tungsten, crew life-support systems designed against known metabolic requirements. The performance figures remain, six decades later, unmatched. Specific impulse values exceeded chemical rockets by orders of magnitude. Mission profiles showed transit times to Mars measured in weeks rather than months. Saturn could be reached in a timeframe shorter than many ocean voyages of the 18th century. The interstellar variant — the one that haunted Freeman Dyson for the rest of his career — could theoretically accelerate to three to five percent of the speed of light. Alpha Centauri within an 80-year journey. A human lifetime. Not a generational abstraction. Could the team have circumvented the treaty by launching from orbit instead? In principle, yes — assembling the ship in space would bypass atmospheric fallout entirely. In practice, lifting 4,000 tonnes of components to orbit using 1960s chemical rockets would have required hundreds of Saturn V launches, at a cost exceeding the Apollo program many times over. The capability simply did not exist. By 1965, DARPA funding ended. The project transitioned to theoretical studies only, then to silence. Documents were classified. Scale models were archived — some eventually reaching the Smithsonian. Test footage of the conventional explosive pusher-plate experiments survived, showing a small metal disc rising on sequential blasts, stable and smooth. Proof of a principle that would never be scaled. The engineers dispersed. Taylor moved on. Dyson returned to Princeton, carrying with him the knowledge that interstellar travel had been, for a brief window, an engineering problem with a known solution. Then, in 1965, a young Air Force analyst reviewing the classified Orion files noticed something in the final design specifications. A secondary application of the pusher-plate geometry that the original team had never publicly discussed. It had nothing to do with propulsion. The file was reclassified immediately. Its contents would not surface for—

=== PART 4 ===

The file resurfaced in 1979, buried inside a declassified batch of DARPA technical assessments. Its contents were not dramatic. They were geometric. The pusher-plate curvature that Taylor had optimized for propulsion — channeling nuclear plasma into directional thrust — also functioned, when inverted, as a shaped-charge focusing element. A warhead geometry capable of concentrating explosive force with unprecedented efficiency. This was the detail that had been reclassified in 1965. Not because it revealed a secret weapon, but because it confirmed what the treaty negotiators had already suspected: Project Orion was never purely civilian. DARPA's original sponsorship in July 1958 had not been motivated solely by interplanetary ambition. A vessel capable of delivering 4,000 tonnes to any orbit within hours was, by definition, a strategic asset. The line between exploration and deterrence had never existed. [THE DOOR THAT CLOSED BOTH WAYS]

The Partial Test Ban Treaty of 1963 did not mention Project Orion by name. It did not need to. By prohibiting nuclear detonations in the atmosphere, in outer space, and underwater, it eliminated every operational environment the propulsion system required. The treaty's authors understood this. Several had been briefed on the program. What followed was not cancellation — it was quiet absorption. The radiation shielding mathematics developed for Orion's crew compartments became foundational to deep-space mission planning for decades afterward. Shock-absorber calculations, designed to translate millisecond nuclear impulses into survivable crew acceleration, informed landing-system engineering for robotic planetary probes. NASA's 1990s-era Project Longshot, an unmanned interstellar probe concept, drew directly on Orion's nuclear pulse principles. DARPA's early-2000s Project Prometheus explored nuclear-electric alternatives that owed their theoretical framing to work completed in San Diego four decades earlier. The technical contributions outlived the program that generated them. This is not unusual in aerospace history. What is unusual is the scale of what was abandoned. [WHAT CAPABILITY MEANS WITHOUT PERMISSION]

Consider the comparison. Apollo cost approximately $25 billion in 1960s dollars and delivered twelve humans to the lunar surface across six successful landings. The projected budget for Orion's first Mars mission — with a crew of 150 and colonization-grade payload — was $24 billion. The budgets were comparable. The ambitions were not. Apollo was incremental: each mission reached slightly farther, stayed slightly longer, returned slightly more data. Orion was categorical. Its first operational flight would have carried more people to Mars than every Apollo mission combined carried to the Moon. But Apollo could fail without existential consequence. A Saturn V explosion, however catastrophic for its crew, did not scatter strontium-90 across a continent. Orion's risk profile was fundamentally incompatible with a public space program operating under democratic oversight. Modern nuclear pulse propulsion studies continue — on paper. Fusion-based variants promise similar performance without fission's radioactive byproducts. None have been funded beyond the conceptual stage. The questions that Orion raised in 1959 remain unanswered in the present: Could international cooperation distribute the risk that no single nation proved willing to accept alone? The gap between technical feasibility and political acceptability can be wider than the distance to Alpha Centauri. Orion proved this with engineering precision. Humanity possessed, by 1962, a validated method for reaching nearby stars within a single human lifetime. That capability was not lost. It was declined. Freeman Dyson returned to Princeton. Ted Taylor moved to other work and, in later years, became an advocate for nuclear disarmament. The designs were archived, preserved in technical reports that anyone with sufficient clearance — and, now, sufficient curiosity — can read. The cosmos does not operate on human timelines. The stars that Orion was designed to reach have not moved appreciably since 1965. They will not move appreciably for millennia. Whether humanity returns to nuclear pulse propulsion, or discovers an alternative path that carries fewer consequences, the destination remains fixed. One question lingers. It is not technical. It is personal. If the capability exists — if the engineering is proven, the physics validated, the blueprints preserved — what cost would you consider acceptable for reaching another star? The pusher plate was never built at full scale. The calculations, however, are still on file. Polished on one side. Scorched on the other.
