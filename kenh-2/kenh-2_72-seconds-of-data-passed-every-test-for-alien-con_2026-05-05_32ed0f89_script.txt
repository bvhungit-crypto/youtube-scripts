TITLE: 72 seconds of data passed every test for alien contact. Here's what nobody talks about.

=== PART 1 ===

A sheet of continuous-feed printer paper. Perforated edges. Faint green stripes running the length of it. Columns of ones and twos and threes — the background noise of the universe, rendered in alphanumeric code.

And then, in one column, six characters that should not be there.

6EQUJ5.

Someone has circled them in red pen. In the margin, in handwriting, a single word.

*Wow!*

That piece of paper is now archived at the Ohio History Connection. The telescope that produced it no longer exists. The central question it raised in 1977 remains unanswered: did a radio telescope in rural Ohio detect a signal from an extraterrestrial intelligence — and if so, why has it never been heard again?

To understand the signal, we first need to understand the instrument.

[THE EAR]

Before there was a signal, there was a field. Flat Ohio farmland near the campus of Ohio Wesleyan University in Delaware, Ohio. In 1963, construction finished on one of the most unusual radio telescopes ever built.

It was called Big Ear.

Most radio telescopes are dishes. Big Ear was not. Two enormous reflectors arranged in sequence — a tiltable flat reflector standing 100 feet tall and stretching 340 feet long, which caught incoming radio waves and directed them onto a fixed parabolic reflector measuring 360 feet long by 70 feet high. Together, these two surfaces gave Big Ear the collecting power and resolution of a conventional dish antenna roughly 157 feet in diameter.

If you laid that equivalent dish flat on the ground, it would cover more area than a football field.

This was not a small instrument. And it was not idle.

From 1963 to 1971, Big Ear conducted a systematic survey of the radio sky. Nearly 20,000 individual radio sources catalogued. Roughly 60 percent of them had never been recorded before. By any measure, the telescope had already justified its existence.

Then its mission changed.

[A NEW ASSIGNMENT]

In 1973, Ohio State University reassigned Big Ear to a different purpose: the scientific search for extraterrestrial intelligence. This was not a fringe project. It became the longest-running SETI program in history, operating continuously from 1973 through 1997 — twenty-four years maintained by volunteer scientists, funded through NASA grants and private donations.

The upgrades were precise. A new 50-channel receiver installed and connected to the observatory's existing IBM 1130 computer. New software, written specifically for the SETI search, designed by a volunteer named Jerry Ehman — an electrical engineer and professor who had joined the project out of scientific curiosity.

What followed was years of listening.

The telescope scanned the sky as Earth rotated beneath it, feeding data into the IBM 1130, which processed the signals and printed the results on continuous-feed paper. Columns of alphanumeric characters. Each character represented the intensity of a radio signal received on a given channel during a given time interval. The numbers 1 through 9 covered low to moderate intensities. If a signal exceeded 9, the system switched to letters — A for 10, B for 11, and so on.

Most of the printout was ones. Twos. The occasional three. Background static from a universe filled with faint, diffuse radio emissions.

By 1977, Ehman had been reviewing these printouts by hand for years. Thousands of pages. Tens of thousands of columns. The work was methodical, repetitive, and — overwhelmingly — uneventful.

On August 19, 1977, he sat down with the data collected four days earlier. August 15. The printout looked like every other. Rows of low numbers, the quiet hum of the cosmos rendered in ink.

And then he saw it.

One column. One channel out of fifty. A sequence that climbed from the background — 6, then E, then Q, then U, then J, then 5. The U alone represented a signal intensity thirty times above the background level.

Nothing in the data had ever looked like this.

Ehman circled the sequence in red pen. He wrote a single word in the margin.

If you are watching this and wondering what those six characters actually mean — what made them so different from the thousands of columns that came before — that is exactly where we need to go next.

=== PART 2 ===

[THE ANATOMY OF SIX CHARACTERS]

The signal lasted exactly 72 seconds.

Not approximately. Seventy-two seconds — the precise duration that any point source in the sky would remain within the Big Ear's beam as the Earth rotated beneath it. This was geometry, not coincidence. And it mattered, because it meant the signal behaved exactly as a source from deep space should behave when observed by a fixed radio telescope on a turning planet.

Big Ear did not track objects across the sky. It was a transit telescope — it stared at a fixed strip of sky and let the Earth's rotation sweep the cosmos through its field of view. Any genuine extraterrestrial source, whether a distant quasar or a transmitting civilization, would enter the beam gradually, peak in intensity as it crossed the center, then fade symmetrically as it drifted out the other side. The resulting intensity profile would form a smooth bell curve over those 72 seconds.

The Wow! signal traced that curve almost perfectly.

Consider the sequence again: 6EQUJ5. Each character represents a measurement of signal intensity sampled at 12-second intervals. The numbers 1 through 9 correspond to intensity levels on a linear scale. When the signal exceeded 9, the system switched to letters. A equals 10. B equals 11. The letter U — the peak of the sequence — represents an intensity value of 30.

Thirty times the background level. On a single channel.

Picture the printout as Ehman would have seen it. Column after column of ones, twos, the occasional three. Background noise. The quiet static of the universe at radio frequencies. Then, in one column out of fifty, a spike that climbed from 6 to 14 to 26 to 30, dropped to 19, then fell back to 5. A signal that rose and fell with the mathematical precision of a source moving through the telescope's beam.

Then vanished entirely.

The other 49 channels recorded nothing unusual. Whatever produced this signal was radiating at an extraordinarily narrow bandwidth, less than 10 kilohertz wide. By comparison, a typical FM radio station broadcasts across a bandwidth of 200 kilohertz — twenty times broader. Natural astrophysical sources tend to emit across wide swaths of the radio spectrum. Pulsars, quasars, interstellar gas clouds — they are noisy, spectrally speaking. They do not confine their energy to a sliver of frequency space.

Engineered transmissions do.

[THE FREQUENCY THAT WAS CHOSEN BEFORE ANYONE CHOSE IT]

The signal arrived at 1420.4556 megahertz.

This number deserves a pause.

Hydrogen is the most abundant element in the universe. When a neutral hydrogen atom undergoes a quantum spin-flip transition — when the spin of its electron reverses relative to its proton — it emits a photon at precisely 1420.405 megahertz. This emission line, known as the hydrogen line or the 21-centimeter line, is one of the most fundamental frequencies in radio astronomy. It is, in a sense, the universe's most common note.

In 1959, eighteen years before Ehman circled those six characters, physicists Philip Morrison and Giuseppe Cocconi published a landmark paper in the journal Nature. Their argument was elegant: if an extraterrestrial civilization wished to announce its presence across interstellar distances, it would choose a frequency that any technologically capable species would already be monitoring. The hydrogen line was the obvious candidate — a universal constant, recognizable to any civilization that had discovered radio astronomy.

The Wow! signal arrived within a fraction of a megahertz of that prediction.

This frequency also sits within what radio astronomers call the "water hole" — a quiet region of the electromagnetic spectrum between the hydrogen line at 1420 MHz and the hydroxyl line at 1662 MHz. Hydrogen and hydroxyl combine to form water. The symbolism was not lost on the scientists who named it. More practically, this band is relatively free of natural interference, making it ideal for both radio astronomy and, hypothetically, interstellar communication. Terrestrial transmitters are forbidden from broadcasting in this range by international agreement.

The signal could not have been a television broadcast, a military radar, or a commercial satellite. Those sources operate at different frequencies, and none are permitted near the hydrogen line. Big Ear's location in rural Ohio in 1977 placed it far from most sources of urban radio interference. The telescope's design inherently filtered out signals originating from the ground.

The direction was equally striking. The signal arrived from the constellation Sagittarius — toward the dense, star-rich center of the Milky Way, where the highest concentration of stellar systems in our galaxy resides.

Here was the situation. A narrowband signal, confined to a single channel, at the frequency that two physicists had predicted would be used for interstellar contact. It rose and fell in intensity over 72 seconds, matching the exact signature of a point source observed by a transit telescope. Its peak was thirty times the background noise level — a signal-to-noise ratio that no natural or terrestrial source detected by Big Ear had ever produced. It came from the most star-dense region of the galaxy. It could not be attributed to any known terrestrial or orbital source.

Every criterion that the SETI program had established for identifying a candidate extraterrestrial signal — every single one — was satisfied.

In the weeks that followed the discovery, astronomers turned Big Ear back toward the coordinates in Sagittarius. They searched the same strip of sky, on the same frequency, with the same equipment that had captured the original detection. The telescope swept through those coordinates again and again, each pass lasting the same 72 seconds that the Earth's rotation allowed.

The sky was silent.

Other observatories joined the effort. Larger dishes, more sensitive receivers, broader spectral coverage — all aimed at the same patch of sky where the signal had originated. They listened for days. Then weeks. Months. The coordinates were revisited with instruments far more powerful than the Big Ear had ever been.

Nothing. Not a whisper. Not a single repetition on any frequency, at any time, from any direction near the original source.

The signal that had passed every test science could design had done something no protocol had anticipated.

It had appeared once, satisfied every criterion for extraterrestrial origin, and then—

=== PART 3 ===

It vanished.

That is the fact that defines everything that followed. Not the detection — the absence. For seventy-two seconds on August 15, 1977, something registered on a single channel of a fifty-channel receiver with an intensity thirty standard deviations above background noise. It matched the expected signature of a point source drifting through the telescope's beam. It occupied a frequency that international law prohibits humans from transmitting on.

And then silence.

[THE DUAL HORN PROBLEM]

Big Ear did not observe the sky the way most telescopes do. Its design employed two feed horns — separate receivers positioned so that as Earth's rotation swept the sky overhead, any persistent source would pass through the first horn's beam, then through the second horn's beam approximately three minutes later. This was not incidental architecture. This was the telescope's built-in verification system.

On the night of August 15, the signal appeared in one horn only.

A continuous source — a pulsar, a quasar, a transmitting beacon — would have registered twice. The three-minute gap between horns was brief enough that any stable emitter should have produced a near-identical detection in the second pass. Whatever generated the signal either ceased transmitting within that window, moved out of the beam's narrow field of view, or was never continuous to begin with.

This single architectural detail eliminated an entire category of explanation. The source was transient. Transient signals, by their nature, resist confirmation.

Ehman understood this immediately. So did his colleagues at Ohio State. What followed was not celebration. It was a systematic campaign to recover something that had already gone silent.

[THE ELIMINATION]

Over the next two decades, the coordinates in Sagittarius were revisited with a persistence that bordered on institutional obsession. Big Ear itself returned to the position repeatedly through the late 1970s and into the 1980s. In 1987, Robert Gray conducted a targeted search using the META array at Harvard. In 1995 and again in 1999, Gray secured time on the Very Large Array in New Mexico — an instrument with vastly greater sensitivity and angular resolution than Big Ear had ever possessed.

Each search produced the same result. Silence.

Meanwhile, terrestrial explanations were tested and discarded with clinical precision. Could the signal have originated from a satellite? The 1420 MHz hydrogen line is internationally protected under radio astronomy regulations. No licensed satellite transmits on or near it. The signal's drift characteristics were inconsistent with orbital motion. Could it have been an aircraft reflection? Aircraft produce broadband interference across multiple channels, not a narrowband spike on a single one. Ground-based radio frequency interference? The signal's intensity profile — its smooth rise and fall over seventy-two seconds — precisely matched the Gaussian response pattern of a celestial source transiting the telescope's beam.

Terrestrial sources, being close, do not produce this signature.

Each proposed explanation failed on at least one critical parameter. Most failed on several.

Then, in 2017, astronomer Antonio Paris offered something new. Two comets — 266P/Christensen and P/2008 Y2 — had been in the general vicinity of the signal's coordinates in August 1977. Comets carry hydrogen. Hydrogen emits at 1420 MHz. For the first time, a natural astrophysical mechanism appeared to match the frequency.

The hypothesis attracted significant media attention. It also attracted scrutiny. When Paris observed the comets directly, no comparable signal was detected. More critically, hydrogen emission from a cometary coma is broadband — it spreads across a wide range of frequencies. The Wow! signal occupied less than 10 kHz of bandwidth. The intensity differential posed an even larger problem: cometary hydrogen emission is faint, orders of magnitude below what Big Ear recorded that night.

The comet hypothesis did not survive peer review.

[THE DEMOLITION]

By the 1990s, the volunteers who had maintained Big Ear's SETI program for nearly a quarter century were aging. Funding had grown sparse. The land beneath the telescope belonged to the university's development office. Developers had plans.

In 1998, the Big Ear Radio Observatory was torn down. The instrument that had catalogued nearly 20,000 radio sources. That had conducted the longest continuous SETI survey in history. That had captured the only signal ever to satisfy every criterion for extraterrestrial origin.

A golf course now occupies the site.

The data survived. Six characters on a printout, archived at the Ohio History Connection. A red circle. A single word in the margin. Thousands of hours of follow-up observations, all negative, stored across institutional databases.

And then, in 2012, the Arecibo Observatory did something no one had attempted before. On the thirty-fifth anniversary of the detection, Arecibo transmitted a coded response — ten thousand Twitter messages and a series of data sequences — directly toward the signal's point of origin in Sagittarius.

That transmission is currently traveling at the speed of light through interstellar space. If anything exists at the source coordinates, our reply will arrive in approximately 25,000 years.

=== PART 4 ===

[WHAT THE SILENCE ESTABLISHED]

The central question of this investigation was direct: what did Jerry Ehman detect on August 15, 1977?

The answer, supported by every piece of available evidence, is equally direct. 

We do not know. 

Not because the data is ambiguous — the signal passed every criterion that Morrison, Cocconi, and subsequent SETI architects established for identifying an artificial extraterrestrial transmission — but because a single unrepeated detection cannot be confirmed. The scientific method requires reproducibility. The Wow! signal provided none. It satisfied the test once, completely, and then offered no opportunity for verification.

This is not a failure of the signal. It is a constraint of the method.

What the Wow! signal revealed extends beyond the question of its origin. It exposed a structural vulnerability in how humanity searches for intelligence beyond Earth. 

Every SETI protocol assumes recurrence — that a detectable signal will persist long enough, or repeat often enough, for independent confirmation. The Wow! signal demonstrated that this assumption may be flawed. If transmissions from another civilization are brief, directional, or singular, then detection becomes a function of coincidence: the right instrument, aimed at the right coordinates, recording at the right moment.

Big Ear happened to be listening. Its second feed horn, scanning the same patch of sky three minutes later, recorded nothing. Had the signal arrived during those three minutes instead, it would have been captured by the offset receiver and missed by the primary one — producing the same ambiguity. Had it arrived six hours earlier or later, the telescope would have been pointed elsewhere entirely.

The window was 72 seconds wide. We were looking for exactly that duration. Then it closed.

Modern SETI programs have absorbed this lesson. The Breakthrough Listen initiative scans millions of channels simultaneously across multiple observatories, archiving raw data for retrospective analysis. Coordinated networks allow near-instantaneous cross-verification. These architectures exist, in part, because of what happened in Delaware, Ohio — because a volunteer scientist reviewing printouts by hand found six characters that matched a theoretical prediction made eighteen years prior, and then watched the source fall silent forever.

The printout survives. It is preserved at the Ohio History Connection — the red circle, the handwritten annotation, the alphanumeric sequence 6EQUJ5 rendered in dot-matrix ink on continuous-feed paper. The coordinates in Sagittarius are still monitored. The Arecibo response, transmitted in 2012, continues its journey outward at the speed of light, covering 5.88 trillion miles per year.

Everything we built to listen was informed by the one time something answered — and the forty-seven years of silence that followed.
