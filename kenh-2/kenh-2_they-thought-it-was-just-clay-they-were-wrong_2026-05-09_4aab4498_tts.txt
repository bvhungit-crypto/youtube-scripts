The molecule detected in Glen Torridon is not a nucleobase. Not identical to any canonical base.

Three explanations remain plausible. The data does not yet distinguish between them.

First, biological production. Ancient microorganisms synthesizing organic compounds in Gale's lake system, molecular remnants entombed in sediment.

Second, abiotic geochemical synthesis. Volcanic heat, hydrothermal fluids, or mineral-surface catalysis generating organics without any living intermediary.

Third, meteoritic delivery. Carbonaceous chondrites and interplanetary dust raining organic material onto Mars over billions of years, accumulating in lake-bottom clay.

All three pathways have precedent elsewhere in the Solar System. Meteorites found on Earth contain nucleobase analogs. Hydrothermal vents on ocean floors produce thiophenes abiotically. Biology generates all these compounds in abundance.

Glen Torridon's clay-rich mudstones formed about three point five billion years ago, when Mars still held standing water and active geochemical cycles.
In Earth's rock record, the oldest widely accepted microfossils, fine, filament-like traces preserved in Western Australian chert, come from essentially the same span of deep time.
Two planets, never closer than roughly fifty-five million kilometers, each had liquid water, volcanic energy sources, and carbon-bearing chemistry within the same geological window.

On one world, biology emerged and persisted.
On the other, the ingredients settled into clay, then remained in place as the environment thinned, cooled, and quieted.

For that, finer discriminators are needed.
Stable carbon isotope ratios, carbon-thirteen relative to carbon-twelve, can, in some contexts, separate biological fractionation from abiotic pathways.

Chirality is another divide, living systems on Earth strongly prefer specific left- or right-handed molecular forms, especially in amino acids and sugars.
No payload aboard Curiosity can perform that chiral separation.
And rigorous cross-checking against terrestrial and meteoritic reference materials requires contamination controls and repeatability standards that mobile rover laboratories cannot provide on an exposed planetary surface.

Earth laboratories operate in a different regime entirely, parts-per-trillion detection limits, sub-permil isotopic resolution, and chiral analysis on nanogram-scale samples are routine.
This is not a small performance gap to be closed by calibration updates.
It is a categorical separation between in-situ reconnaissance and full forensic chemistry.

What is now anchored to direct measurement, not inference: Gale Crater hosted a persistent lake system. Fine-grained clay sediments accumulated there in calm water. Those sediments incorporated chemically diverse organics, thiophenes, aromatic hydrocarbons, sulfur-bearing species, and structures near known biochemical building blocks. Mars therefore reads less like a sterile plain and more like an archive whose pages are weathered but still intact. The record can be sampled. Parts of it can already be read in place. Yet the decisive interpretation, biology, prebiotic chemistry, or both, sits beyond rover-scale capability. To resolve that final ambiguity, selected cores must travel about fifty-five million kilometers, at minimum transfer distance, into laboratories built for the necessary precision.

At first glance, Glen Torridon remains visually ordinary, gray rock, fine laminations, dust caught in shallow texture. Nothing about the outcrop announces a chemical library. What changed is not appearance. What changed is inventory, and the clear recognition that the last interpretive step cannot be taken on Mars with current tools.

So the remaining question has shifted. It is no longer simply whether ancient Mars could have supported life. It is whether the answer, if present, has already been sealed in clay for billions of years, waiting for the moment those samples finally arrive on Earth.
