Jupiter contains more mass than every other planet in the Solar System combined, twice over.
Before Juno, one spacecraft had orbited it.
Galileo arrived in nineteen ninety-five.

It circled for eight years, powered by nuclear batteries, tracing an equatorial path that mapped moons and measured the magnetosphere.
It never flew over the poles.
It never mapped the gravitational field with the precision required to read what lay beneath the clouds.

That gap left the most fundamental question in planetary science unanswered.
What is actually inside the largest planet we have ever known?

Jupiter's gravitational signature is the only tool we have to determine its interior structure.
Measure the pull from enough angles, with enough precision, and mass distribution reveals itself.
Galileo's equatorial orbit could not provide that resolution.

The poles remained unmapped.
The core remained theoretical.

So the assumption persisted.
A solid seed, rock and ice, roughly ten times Earth's mass, accreting hydrogen and helium over millions of years until it became the gas giant observed today.
This is core accretion.

It does not apply only to Jupiter.
It is the dominant framework for how every gas giant in the universe forms.

Jupiter was the anchor case, and if its interior was wrong... the formation model built on it was wrong for every gas giant in the universe.

Juno launched from Cape Canaveral on August five, two thousand and eleven.
It carried no nuclear power source.
Every spacecraft sent to the outer Solar System before it had relied on radioisotope thermoelectric generators.

Juno ran on sunlight.
Three solar panel wings, each the size of a tractor-trailer, the largest ever deployed on a planetary probe, would generate power at a distance where the Sun delivers four percent of the energy it provides at Earth.

The margins were narrow.
Built by Lockheed Martin, operated by Nasa's Jet Propulsion Laboratory, the spacecraft carried nine instruments sealed inside a titanium radiation vault rated to withstand the equivalent of one hundred million dental X-rays.
A cumulative dose roughly four hundred thousand times the annual radiation limit considered safe for a human worker.

The mission cost one point four six billion dollars.
The orbit design was unprecedented.
Polar passes skimming five thousand kilometers above the cloud tops, threading the gap between Jupiter and its radiation belts, then retreating to safer distance on a fifty-three-day elliptical loop.

Each close pass would last hours.
Each retreat would last weeks.
Minimize exposure, maximize data, survive.

On July five, two thousand and sixteen, after five years in transit, Juno fired its main engine for thirty-five minutes and entered Jupiter's orbit.
Telemetry confirmed stable trajectory.
Solar panels locked and generating power.
Radiation shielding held.

Orbital insertion, the most dangerous moment of the mission, was complete.

The gravity measurements would come next.
Collected during the second orbit, they would map mass distribution inside the planet with a precision Galileo never approached.
Scientists prepared their models.
They expected confirmation.

The first gravity data arrived in August two thousand and sixteen.
The numbers were fed into simulations built on decades of theoretical work.
The models did not converge.

The northern hemisphere pulled differently than the southern.
Mass was distributed in ways no compact core could explain.

They ran the calculations again.

Same result.

Jupiter's core was not solid.
It was not distinct.
It was not what two centuries of planetary science had predicted.

Something inside the largest planet in the Solar System differed fundamentally from what every model assumed, and if Jupiter's interior was wrong... the formation theory built on it required revision.

The data was just the beginning.

The gravitational field told a story no existing model could accommodate.
Jupiter's northern hemisphere exerted a measurably different pull than its southern.
The asymmetry was pronounced enough that a compact, well-defined core could not produce it.

Scientists constructed interior models capable of generating these specific gravitational harmonics.
Only one configuration fit, a core that was not a discrete sphere of rock and metal, but a vast, diffuse region where heavy elements, silicates, iron, water ice, blended gradually into the surrounding hydrogen and helium envelope.

This region extended outward through roughly thirty to fifty percent of Jupiter's radius, between approximately twenty-one thousand and thirty-five thousand kilometers from the planet's center, a span nearly three times the diameter of Earth.
That is not a core in any conventional sense.
It is a gradient.

The standard model of gas giant formation depends on a process called core accretion.
A solid seed of approximately ten Earth masses aggregates from dust and ice in the protoplanetary disk.
Once that seed reaches critical mass, it captures hydrogen and helium from the surrounding nebula, rapidly inflating into a gas giant.

The seed should persist, compressed, dense, distinct, buried beneath seventy thousand kilometers of atmosphere and metallic hydrogen.
Every simulation of Jupiter's formation had predicted it.

Juno's gravity data contradicted it.

Two hypotheses emerged.
The first proposed a giant impact, early in the Solar System's history, a planetary embryo roughly ten times Earth's mass may have collided with the young Jupiter, delivering enough kinetic energy to shatter and disperse its primordial core.

Simulations published in Nature in two thousand and nineteen showed that such an impact could produce exactly the dilute core structure Juno observed, heavy elements stirred outward, mixed irreversibly into the metallic hydrogen mantle.

The second hypothesis required no collision at all.
Under the extreme pressures found deep inside Jupiter, exceeding forty million atmospheres, rock and ice may dissolve into metallic hydrogen the way salt dissolves into warm water.
If dissolution rates exceeded settling rates, the core would never have fully consolidated.
It would have remained fuzzy from the beginning.

Both explanations carry consequences that extend far beyond Jupiter.
Over five thousand exoplanets have been confirmed to date, as of two thousand and twenty-three, approximately one thousand, seven hundred of those are classified as gas giants or Neptune-like worlds whose bulk properties are inferred using the same core accretion model.

If that model's foundational assumption, a solid, persistent core, does not hold for the gas giant closest to us... the interior structures assigned to distant worlds may require systematic revision.

Let that settle for a moment.

Juno's polar orbits also mapped Jupiter's magnetic field at a resolution Galileo never achieved.
The field was not dipolar in the clean, symmetric way Earth's field approximates.
Instead, it was lopsided, the northern hemisphere hosted a concentrated region of intense magnetic flux, a localized patch where field lines converged, while the southern hemisphere exhibited a broader, more diffuse pattern.

The magnetic equator was tilted and offset from the rotational equator.
This asymmetry implied that the dynamo generating the field, convective churning of electrically conductive metallic hydrogen, was itself asymmetric.
The interior was not a uniform fluid engine.
It was structured, layered, and unevenly active in ways that no pre-Juno model had anticipated.

By contrast, the atmosphere had been assumed to be better understood.
Decades of telescopic observation and data from the Galileo probe's descent in nineteen ninety-five had established a layered model, ammonia ice clouds at the top, ammonium hydrosulfide below, water clouds deeper still.
Orderly.
Stratified.
Predictable.

Juno's microwave radiometer changed that picture entirely.
Operating at six wavelengths, the instrument could peer through cloud layers opaque to visible light, measuring thermal emissions from depths extending three hundred and fifty kilometers below the visible cloud tops.
What it revealed was not orderly stratification.

Ammonia concentrations deviated sharply from predicted values.
In equatorial bands, measured levels fell to roughly one-third of expected solar abundance, while polar regions showed enrichment, and mid-latitudes remained patchy and irregular.
The neat three-layer model dissolved into something far more turbulent and dynamic.

The depletion was the puzzle.
Ammonia is abundant in Jupiter's atmosphere, roughly three times the solar abundance.
It should be well-mixed at depth, uniformly distributed by convection.

Instead, vast equatorial regions showed ammonia levels far below what thermochemical equilibrium predicted.
Something was physically removing ammonia from the upper atmosphere and transporting it downward.

The explanation involved a mechanism no one had applied to Jupiter before.
Weather.
Specifically... hail.

Ammonia acts as a powerful antifreeze.
When it encounters water vapor at temperatures between one hundred and seventy-three and two hundred and thirty-three Kelvin, conditions found in Jupiter's upper cloud layers, it dissolves into the water, forming ammonia-water solution droplets.
These droplets are denser than the surrounding hydrogen-helium atmosphere.
They sink.

In calm conditions, they evaporate at warmer depths, releasing ammonia back into the gas.
However, inside vigorous storm updrafts, the droplets collide and merge, growing into slushy hailstones several centimeters across.
Scientists named them mushballs.

Once large enough, mushballs fall faster than updrafts can support them, plunging hundreds of kilometers through the atmosphere before finally evaporating at depths where temperatures exceed four hundred Kelvin.

This single mechanism explained the ammonia depletion, the latitudinal variation, and the deep enrichment simultaneously.
It also meant that Jupiter's atmosphere operates on principles familiar from terrestrial meteorology: condensation, precipitation, convective transport, scaled to a planet with no surface, no ocean, and atmospheric depths exceeding the diameter of Earth.

The physics is the same.
The canvas is incomparably larger.

Which brings us to the feature that has defined Jupiter in the human imagination for over three centuries.
The Great Red Spot was first reliably documented in the 1830s, though observations possibly dating to sixteen sixty-five suggest it may be far older.
At its peak in the late 1800s, the storm spanned roughly forty thousand kilometers, wide enough to swallow three Earths side by side.

Today it measures approximately sixteen thousand kilometers across.
Still larger than our entire planet, but visibly diminished.

What no telescope could determine was how deep it reached.
Was the Great Red Spot a shallow atmospheric eddy, confined to the upper cloud deck?
Or did it extend downward into Jupiter's interior, rooted in processes far below the visible surface?

Juno answered this during multiple close passes.
Gravity measurements detected a mass anomaly associated with the storm, the rotating column of gas was dense enough to perturb the spacecraft's trajectory.
Microwave radiometer data confirmed thermal signatures extending well below the cloud tops.

The Great Red Spot reaches at least three hundred kilometers into Jupiter's atmosphere.
Some analyses suggest depths approaching five hundred kilometers.
For comparison, the most powerful hurricanes on Earth extend roughly fifteen kilometers from sea level to tropopause.

The Great Red Spot is twenty to thirty times deeper.

That depth has a consequence. Jupiter radiates approximately one point six seven times more energy than it receives from the Sun, residual heat from gravitational contraction during formation, supplemented by energy released as helium droplets rain through the metallic hydrogen layer.

The Great Red Spot taps into this internal heat reservoir. Unlike terrestrial hurricanes, which die when severed from warm ocean water, the Great Red Spot draws energy from below.
There is no land to cross, no surface to disrupt the circulation. The storm feeds continuously on a planet-wide heat engine that has operated for four point six billion years.

Yet it is shrinking. Observations spanning two centuries confirm a steady contraction, from three Earth diameters to one point three.
Juno's data added a counterintuitive detail. As the storm narrows, its wind speeds are increasing.

The vortex is tightening, spinning faster as it contracts, like an ice skater pulling in their arms. Whether this acceleration will stabilize the storm at a smaller size or hasten its eventual dissipation remains an open question.

We may be watching a centuries-long death. But the storm is not dying quietly.

And then Juno turned its instruments toward Jupiter's poles, regions no spacecraft had ever imaged in detail, and found something no atmospheric model had predicted. Geometric arrangements of cyclones, locked in stable formations, defying every expectation of turbulent fluid dynamics.
The patterns raised a question the mission team could not yet answer.

Eight cyclones in the north. Five in the south. Winds exceeding three hundred and fifty kilometers per hour. Stable for years.

No model predicted this.

When Juno's infrared instruments mapped Jupiter's poles for the first time, the expectation was turbulence, chaotic, formless convection at the boundaries where the planet's rapid rotation meets its atmosphere.
The data showed something else. Order.

Each polar cyclone spans thousands of kilometers. Comparable in size to the continental United States. They rotate around a central vortex in fixed geometric arrangements, maintaining their positions like dancers locked in choreography.

The forces holding them apart, preventing them from merging into a single massive storm, as fluid dynamics would typically predict, involve anticyclonic rings between each cyclone. A stable equilibrium.
The mechanism is understood in principle.

Why it produces an octagon at one pole and a pentagon at the other is not.

That asymmetry echoes through the entire planet.

Juno's magnetometer mapped Jupiter's magnetic field with a precision no previous mission had achieved. The result contradicted standard dynamo theory.
The field is not symmetric.

In the northern hemisphere, it is roughly twice as strong as in the southern, a disparity so pronounced that it implies the convective currents of metallic hydrogen generating the field are themselves unevenly distributed within Jupiter's interior.

Connect this to the dilute core discovered in the gravity data. A picture emerges... the planet's deep interior is not uniform.
Whatever process smeared heavy elements outward from the core also disrupted the conditions producing the magnetic dynamo.

One feature stood out. Scientists identified a concentrated patch of intense magnetic flux near Jupiter's equator, informally called the Great Blue Spot.
Not a visible storm, but a magnetic anomaly with no counterpart on Earth. Its field strength exceeds anything measured on our planet by orders of magnitude.

Over successive orbits, Juno detected that this feature is drifting eastward. Evidence that Jupiter's magnetic field is actively evolving on timescales of years, not millennia.

The magnetosphere extends over five thousand Jupiter radii on the side facing away from the Sun, the largest coherent structure in the Solar System.
If you could see it from Earth, it would span an area of sky larger than the full Moon.
Within this vast magnetic bubble, charged particles are accelerated to extraordinary energies.

The primary source of those particles is not the solar wind.
It is a moon.

Jupiter's auroras are the most powerful in the Solar System, releasing hundreds of times more energy than Earth's.
Their driver is fundamentally different.
Earth's auroras are powered primarily by interactions between the solar wind and our magnetosphere.
Jupiter generates its own fuel.

Io, the innermost Galilean moon, ejects approximately one ton of sulfur dioxide and other volcanic gases per second into space.
This material ionizes, forming a doughnut-shaped torus of plasma encircling Jupiter along Io's orbital path.
The planet's rapid rotation, one full turn every nine hours and fifty-six minutes, flings this plasma outward through centrifugal force, dragging magnetic field lines with it.

Electrons spiral along those field lines toward the poles at velocities approaching a significant fraction of the speed of light.

When they slam into Jupiter's upper atmosphere, they produce auroral emissions across the electromagnetic spectrum.
Ultraviolet. X-ray. Infrared. Radio.
The energy released continuously exceeds the total power consumption of human civilization.

By contrast, Earth's entire auroral output during a major geomagnetic storm amounts to a fraction of what Jupiter produces in a quiet moment.

Let that comparison settle.

The question was specific, what lies at Jupiter's core?

After one point seven billion miles and more than three terabits of data, Juno provided its answer.
Jupiter does not possess a compact, solid core.
Its center is a dilute region, rock, ice, metal, dissolved into the surrounding hydrogen and helium.
This diffuse zone extends outward through thirty to fifty percent of the planet's radius.
The gravitational harmonics measured during each close pass confirmed this structure repeatedly.
Independent instruments. Independent analytical teams. Same result.

Two centuries of planetary formation theory assumed a dense seed at the heart of every gas giant.

That assumption was incorrect.

The core exists.
But it is diffuse, gradient, fundamentally unlike anything the standard accretion model predicted.
This single finding restructures how planetary scientists understand giant planet formation, not only within this Solar System, but across the thousands of exoplanetary systems catalogued to date.

If Jupiter's core was disrupted by a giant impact during the first tens of millions of years after formation, then violent collisions between planetary embryos were common enough to reshape even the largest bodies in a young stellar system.
If the core never fully solidified, remaining partially dissolved under pressures exceeding forty million atmospheres and temperatures above twenty thousand Kelvin, then the physics governing deep planetary interiors requires models that do not yet exist.

Either explanation demands revision.
Both carry consequences for every gas giant ever classified by mass and radius alone.

Juno continues to orbit Jupiter.
Its trajectory is gradually decaying.
When the spacecraft's systems can no longer maintain stable operations, projected between two thousand and twenty-five and two thousand and twenty-six, mission controllers will execute a deliberate deorbit into Jupiter's atmosphere.
The purpose is planetary protection.
Preventing any possibility that Juno could eventually collide with Europa or Ganymede, where subsurface oceans represent the Solar System's most credible environments for extraterrestrial biology.

The data already collected will occupy researchers for years beyond the mission's end.

Over one hundred peer-reviewed papers have been published from Juno's findings.
Hundreds of scientists across dozens of institutions continue to refine models of Jupiter's interior convection, magnetic dynamo evolution, atmospheric chemistry.
Yet specific questions remain unresolved.

The exact mechanism that diluted the core, impact disruption versus thermodynamic dissolution, has not been conclusively determined.
The three-dimensional structure of the Great Red Spot below the three hundred-kilometer depth is unconstrained.
The forces maintaining the geometric cyclone arrangements at both poles lack a complete theoretical framework.
The magnetic field's pronounced north-south asymmetry implies interior dynamics that no current simulation fully reproduces.

Successor missions are already in transit.
Esa's Juice spacecraft, launched in April two thousand and twenty-three, will arrive at Jupiter in two thousand and thirty-one to conduct detailed studies of Ganymede, Callisto, and Europa.
Nasa's Europa Clipper, launched in October two thousand and twenty-four, will perform nearly fifty close flybys of Europa.

It will map ice shell thickness, currently estimated between fifteen and twenty-five kilometers, and search for evidence of recent geological activity on the surface.
Both missions build directly on Juno's orbital data, using its gravitational and magnetic field maps as baseline references.

Juno was not designed to overturn planetary science.

It was designed to measure Jupiter's gravitational field, magnetic field, and atmospheric composition with precision no prior mission could achieve.
The instruments performed exactly as intended.
The planet simply did not conform to expectation.

A dilute core.
Ammonia-water hailstones falling through hydrogen clouds.
Cyclones locked in polygonal symmetry at the poles.
A storm column plunging three hundred kilometers into an atmosphere with no floor.
None of these phenomena appeared in pre-mission predictions.

Each emerged from measurement alone.

This is the structural pattern Juno reveals.
Gas giants are not static, layered spheres whose interiors can be inferred from surface observations.
They are dynamic systems shaped by violent formation histories, sustained by internal heat, governed by fluid processes operating under conditions no laboratory on Earth can replicate.

Jupiter is eleven point two times wider than Earth.
Its mass exceeds that of all other Solar System planets combined.
Humanity has studied it with telescopes for four centuries.
Visited it with spacecraft for five decades.

It is still not what we thought it was.
